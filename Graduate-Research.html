Overview of Masters Research

My degree is a two-year program, with most of my research happening in the first year. I'll be based in Corvallis, Oregon, and travelling to Nome, Alaska, and the Falkland Islands to conduct my research, marked on the map below. The purpose of this research is to:
  • Evaluate and quantify the presence of invasive Pacific salmon in the Arctic (Nome, Alaska) and SW Atlantic (Falkland Islands)
  • Create a timeline and explanation for bioinvasions of Pacific salmon species in north Alaska and Falkland Islands
  • Evaluate, compare, and contrast the qualitative and quantitative socioeconomic and ecological impacts of Pacific salmon bioinvasions in Arctic and SW Atlantic
  • Compare the narratives of “natural” (range-expansion) vs. “unnatural” (introduced) bioinvasions with a focus on local perspectives, media representations, and policy decisions
  • Create a useful, public-facing overview of the project and findings, specifically tailored to the different communities’ needs and interests

Nome, Alaska: Summer 2023

Due to recent rising temperatures in the Pacific Northwest and Alaska, populations of Pink salmon (Oncorhynchus gorbuscha) have migrated further north, establishing a relatively stable fisheries in Nome. Nome has previously had other strong fisheries, including Chum salmon (Oncorhynchus keta), but climate change and changing land use practices have caused these populations to decline. This part of my research will examine the socioeconomic and ecological effects of a climate-drive bioinvasive fish species as a replacement for other typical fisheries due to the similarity of Pink salmon to the other salmonids that are declining.

Stanley, Falkland Islands: Winter 2024

My research in the Falkland Islands will focus on human introduction of fish species, policies surrounding invasive fish, and a recent population of Coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch) that has migrated around the tip of South America into the Atlantic and Falkland Islands likely due to climate change. This aspect of the project will examine the socioeconomic and ecological ramifications of a new genus of fish (specifically a Pacific genus in the Atlantic) as a climate-driven and introduced bioinvader for a small island nation heavily reliant on fisheries.
